        # Polish pronunciation

        <SeeAlso>[Polish phonology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polish_phonology)</SeeAlso>
        
        This is my attempt to describe Polish phonology and phonotactics. The Wikipedia article has a lot more detail, though.

        This mainly applies to contexts where there are native words. Loanwords that are unchanged from the original spelling are pronounced regarding the origin language's pronunciation rules. Thus, *weekend* is pronounced like in English, but *Waszyngton* (Washington) is pronounced with a V sound instead of a W sound.

        IPA transcriptions for phonemes (annotated /.../) are provided, but I tried to avoid going into phones (annotated [...]) as much as possible. Graphemes are annotated ‚ü®...‚ü©.

        ## Alphabet

        The Polish alphabet extends the Latin alphabet, and has 32 letters. Q, V, and X are only in loanwords. Providing it makes it easier to understand the phonology.

        ```
        A ńĄ B C ńÜ D E ńė F G H I J K L ŇĀ M N ŇÉ O √ď P R S Ňö T U W Y Z ŇĻ ŇĽ
        a ńÖ b c ńá d e ńô f g h i j k l Ňā m n ŇĄ o √≥ p r s Ňõ t u w y z Ňļ Ňľ
        ```

        > **Fun fact!** X actually used to be used in native words, e.g. *xińÖdz* ("priest"). X was removed from the core alphabet at some point, and was replaced with *ks* or *gz*, depending on the pronunciation, such as in *ksińÖdz*, *egzamin* ("exam").

        ## Stress

        Most words are stressed on the penultimate syllable (as in, the 2nd syllable, but counting from the end). However, there are cases where the stress is on the 3rd or 4th syllable from the end.

        - 2nd syllable from the end:
            - single words not indicated below
            - in prepositional phrases with a personal pronoun: ***do** niej* ('to her'), ***na** nas* ('on us'), *prze**ze** mnie* ('because of me')
        - 3rd syllable from the end:
            - words from classical languages:
                - words ending with *-ika*, *-yka*: ***ryt**mika* ("rhythmics"), ***mu**zyka* ("music")
                - some longer words (only in the nominative singular, and no derived words with native affixes): *uni**wer**sytet* ("university")
            - 1st- and 2nd-person plural past forms: *czy**ta**liŇõmy* ("to read": 1-PL-PAST), *czy**ta**liŇõcie* ("to read": 2-PL-PAST)
            - 3rd-person plural conditional forms: *czy**ta**liby* ("to read": 3-VIR-PL-COND), *czy**ta**Ňāyby* ("to read": 3-NVIR-PL-COND)
        - 4th syllable from the end:
            - 1st- and 2nd-person plural conditional forms: *czy**ta**libyŇõmy* ("to read": 1-VIR-PL-COND), *czy**ta**ŇāybyŇõcie* ("to read": 2-NVIR-PL-COND)

        > **Fun fact!** Polish used to use the /…ę/ sound (same as the dark L in English, but as a separate phoneme), but as [delateralization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Delateralization) took its course, it shifted to /w/. Despite this, it is still written ŇĀ, a letter suggesting similarity to L.
        > 
        > We have a name for this shift: [*waŇāczenie*](https://pl.wikipedia.org/wiki/WaŇāczenie). This is a fairly old phenomenon, which started with 16th-century peasants. It was rejected by the upper class, and the stigma lasted until around the 1950s. It was described by realizing ŇĀ as a "non-syllabic U". Today, pronouncing ŇĀ as […ę] is considered conservative.
        > 
        > Some other Slavic languages, such as Bulgarian, are reportedly having that same L-vocalization process.

        > **Fun fact!** RZ used to be a fricative R (/rŐĽ/), but has merged into ŇĽ phonemically. This is another leftover of old Slavic languages, preserved in Czech and Slovak. In other Slavic languages, it has become R instead.
        > 
        > In modern Polish, this is preserved in inflection and derivation, where RZ can become R and vice-versa, e.g. *powiet**rz**e* ("air") and *wiat**r*** ("wind").
        > 
        > In dialects with [mazuration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mazuration), chiefly the Mazovian and Lesser Poland dialects, ‚ü®Ňľ‚ü© is pronounced /z/, but ‚ü®rz‚ü© is pronounced / ź/. Therefore, *morze* ("sea") and *moŇľe* ("maybe"), which are homophones in standard Polish, are not homophones in those dialects.

        ## Phonotactics

        The Polish syllable structure appears to be (C)(C)(C)(C)V(C)(C)(C), as exemplified by the words *wstrzńÖs* ("shock, impact") and *rejestr* ("register" [noun]).

        **Final obstruents are devoiced:** *dŇāug* ("debt") ‚Üí /dwuk/. Inflection explains the spelling: *dŇāug-‚ąÖ* ‚Üí *dŇāug-u*.

        Some words have difficult consonant clusters in the middle that get simplified: *szeŇõńáset* ("six hundred") ‚Üí / ā…õ…ē.s…õt/ or / ā…õj.s…õt/, *jabŇāko* ("apple") ‚Üí /jap.ko/. Inflection and derivation often explains the spelling of those consonant clusters: *sze**Ňõńás**et* ‚Üí *sze**Ňõci**u**s**et*, *ja**bŇāk**o* ‚Üí *ja**bŇā**usz**k**o*.

        ### Assimilation

        - Voiced obstruents right before voiceless consonants become voiceless: *ksińÖŇľka* (book) ‚Üí /k…ē…ĒwŐÉ āka/.
        - In the onset, voiced obstruents right after voiceless consonants become voiceless: *przemiana* (transformation) ‚Üí /p ā…õ.mja.na/.
        - In the middle of a word, voiceless obstruents immediately before voiced obstruents become voiced: *proŇõba* (request) ‚Üí /pr…Ē Ď.ba/.
            - This is the only instance when a phonemic /…£/ may appear: *klechda* (legend) ‚Üí /kl…õ…£.da/.
            - Unlike in English, this kind of assimilation does not happen before sonorants: *tandetny* (trashy) ‚Üí /tan.d…õt.n…ė/.
            - While *rz* and *Ňľ* normally represent the same phoneme, *rz* **cannot cause regressive assimilation** and will always be subject to progressive assimilation, including in the middle of a word. Compare *pokrzywa* ("nettle") ‚Üí /p…Ē.k ā…ė.va/ and *takŇľe* ("also, too") ‚Üí /ta…°. ź…õ/.
        
        ### Onset consonant clusters

For this reason, Poles often insert a very short vowel between the consonants in everyday speech. This shows a fossilized trace of otherwise reduced [yers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yer), very short vowels that existed in Proto-Slavic, marked with \*—Ć/\*ń≠ and \*—ä/\*Ň≠.
