        # Polish pronunciation

        <SeeAlso>[Polish phonology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polish_phonology)</SeeAlso>
        
        This is my attempt to describe Polish phonology and phonotactics.

        ## Alphabet

        The Polish alphabet extends the Latin alphabet, and has 32 letters. Q, V, and X are only in loanwords.

        ```
        A Ą B C Ć D E Ę F G H I J K L Ł M N Ń O Ó P R S Ś T U W Y Z Ź Ż
        a ą b c ć d e ę f g h i j k l ł m n ń o ó p r s ś t u w y z ź ż
        ```

        > **Fun fact!** X actually used to be used, e.g. *xiądz* (priest). X was removed from the core alphabet at some point, and was replaced with *ks* (or *gz* in some cases), so this example is nowadays spelled *ksiądz*.

        ## Pronunciation

        This mainly applies to contexts where there are native words. Loanwords that are unchanged from the original spelling are pronounced regarding the origin language's pronunciation rules. Thus, *weekend* is pronounced like in English, but *Waszyngton* (Washington) is pronounced with a V sound instead of a W sound.

        Most words are stressed on the penultimate syllable.

        ### Vowels
        All vowel sounds are consistent, short, and never distinguished by length:
        - A - as in *back*
        - E - as in *left*
        - I - as Y in *very*
        - O - as in *wrong*
        - Ó/U - as OO in *boot*
        - Y - as I in *thin*
        - Ą and Ę are just O and E, but pronounced nasally, like in French *chanson* (song) or *italien* (Italian).

        Ą and Ę may shift into a single oral vowel or into a V(C) structure, where *C* is *m*, *n*, or *ń*.

        > **Fun fact!** Polish is the only Slavic language to have preserved nasal vowels.

        ### Consonants

        - C is pronounced like TS in *parts*.
        - Ć, Ś, Ź, DŹ are pronounced like CH in *chip*, SH in *ship*, G in *regime*, and J in *jinn*, respectively. Those are iotized. The same applies to CI, SI, ZI, and DZI in most cases.
        - CZ, SZ, Ż, DŻ are pronounced like CH in *chop*, SH in *show*, S in *vision*, and J in *jar*, respectively. Those are not iotized.
        - G is always a hard G.
        - H and CH are pronounced a lot like H in English, but lean closer to K. (Personally, I can't really distinguish between the two sounds.)
        - J is pronounced like Y in *yen*.
        - Ł is pronounced like W in *week*.
        - Ń is pronounced like NY in *canyon*.
        - RZ is pronounced like Ż (see above).
        - S is always devoiced, so it's pronounced like in *sock*.
        - T never becomes a tongue flap, so it's pronounced like in the word *tap*!
        - W is pronounced like V in *vine*.
        - All other consonants are pronounced like you'd expect if you spoke English, that is: B, D, F, K, L, M, N, P, Z.

        > **Fun fact!** Many other Slavic languages cannot distinguish between /v/ and /w/, so they only end up using /v/, even in loanwords. Polish used to use the /ɫ/ sound (same as the dark L in English), but as [delateralization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Delateralization) took its course, it shifted to /w/. The orthography and several dialects still using /ɫ/ are evidence of this. Additionally, loanwords (like place names) that are spelled with [w] but pronounced /w/ shift it to /v/ when inflected: *Okinawa*, pronounced /ɔki'nawa/ or /ɔki'nava/, becomes *na Okinawie* /na ɔki'navʲɛ/.

        ### Phonotactics

        **Final obstruents:** They are devoiced: *dług* (debt) → *dłuk*. It is still written with a *g* because of inflection: *dług-ø* → *dług-u*. This happens even if succeeded by a consonant with no unvoiced counterpart: *bóbr* (beaver) → *bópr*, but written *bóbr* because of *bobra*.  

        **Consonant clusters (not onset):** is common to simplify some difficult consonant clusters in the middle of a word: *kostka* (dice, ankle) → *kos-ka*, *sześćset* (six hundred) → *sześ-set* OR *szej-set*. In those cases, the word's inflected forms, or other related words, explain the spelling, as can be seen in the genitive: *ko**stk**a* → *ko**st**e**k***, *sze**śćs**et* → *sze**ści**u**s**et*.

        #### Assimilation
        
        - Voiced obstruents right before voiceless consonants become voiceless: *książka* (book) → *ksiąszka*.
        - Voiced obstruents right after voiceless consonants in the onset become voiceless: *przemiana* (transformation) → *pszemiana*.
        - Voiceless obstruents immediately before voiced obstruents in the middle of a word become voiced: *prośba* (request) → *proź-ba*.
        
        #### Onset consonant clusters
        
        Idiosyncracies like *rtęć* (mercury) or *źdźbło* (blade [of a grass or cereal]) are mostly preserved for historical reasons. For this reason, Poles often insert a very short vowel between the consonants in everyday speech.
