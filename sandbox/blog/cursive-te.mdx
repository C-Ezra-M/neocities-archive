If you look at any handwritten text in Russian or another language that uses a similar alphabet, you may notice that Te looks like a lowercase Latin "m". This can also apply to italic text in those languages, since it is often used in computer fonts.

But why this form? We need to look back to a letter that is no longer used in modern Slavic languages: omega (Ѡ ѡ). It is directly based on the Greek lowercase omega (ω).

Why is this important? We'd need to consider the only word it was used for in Old Church Slavonic: ѡтъ *otŭ* ("from, because of") and the prefix ѡтъ-. With time, scribes began inventing ligatures to save space in manuscripts, and Ot (Ѿ ѿ) was one of them. Later it was considered a separate letter of the Cyrillic alphabet.

Ot ѿ is similar to how Te is sometimes written: like Sha with a stroke on top. Over time, Ot stopped being a discrete letter of the alpahbet. This convention still exists in Serbia and North Macedonia. The convention in Bulgarian and in East Slavic languages became to write a reversed Sha instead.

There is potential for confusion without the extra stroke. For example, Russian тиши *tiši*, an inflected form of тишь *tiš'* ("quiet, silence, calm"), can be written in such a way that the boundaries between I and Sha are not easily seen, unless the letters are written separately. Or птица *ptica* ("bird"), where Pe and Te use similar shapes, so do I and Tse.
